Understanding Volumes of Revolution

Welcome to this interactive guide on calculating volumes of revolution using the Disk and Washer methods.

In calculus, we often want to find the volume of a solid that is created by rotating a region around an axis. Two common techniques for calculating these volumes are:

  • Disk Method: Used when the region is rotated around an axis that forms one of the boundaries of the region.
  • Washer Method: Used when the region is rotated around an axis that does not intersect with the region.

This website provides interactive visualizations to help understand these concepts. You can:

The Disk Method

Theory

The disk method calculates the volume of a solid of revolution by slicing the solid into thin disks perpendicular to the axis of rotation.

When we rotate the region between \(y = f(x)\) and the x-axis around the x-axis, the volume is:

\[ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} [f(x)]^2 \, dx \]

Similarly, when rotating around the y-axis, the volume is:

\[ V = \pi \int_{c}^{d} [g(y)]^2 \, dy \]

where \(g(y)\) is the function expressing x in terms of y.

The Washer Method

Theory

The washer method is used when the region being rotated doesn't touch the axis of rotation, resulting in a hole in the middle of the solid.

When we rotate the region between \(y = f(x)\) and \(y = g(x)\) around the x-axis (where \(f(x) \geq g(x)\)), the volume is:

\[ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} [f(x)^2 - g(x)^2] \, dx \]

And when rotating around the y-axis:

\[ V = \pi \int_{c}^{d} [f_1(y)^2 - f_2(y)^2] \, dy \]

where \(f_1(y)\) and \(f_2(y)\) are the outer and inner functions expressed in terms of y.

Example Problems

Example 1: Disk Method

Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by \(y = x^2\), \(y = 0\), and \(x = 2\) around the x-axis.

Solution:

Since we're rotating around the x-axis, we'll use the disk method with the formula:

\[ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} [f(x)]^2 \, dx \]

Here, \(f(x) = x^2\), \(a = 0\), and \(b = 2\).

\begin{align} V &= \pi \int_{0}^{2} (x^2)^2 \, dx\\ &= \pi \int_{0}^{2} x^4 \, dx\\ &= \pi \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_{0}^{2}\\ &= \pi \left( \frac{32}{5} - 0 \right)\\ &= \frac{32\pi}{5} \approx 20.11 \end{align}

Example 2: Washer Method

Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by \(y = x^2\), \(y = x\), and \(x \geq 0\) around the y-axis.

Solution:

Since we're rotating around the y-axis, we'll use the washer method. First, we need to find where the two functions intersect:

\begin{align} x^2 &= x\\ x^2 - x &= 0\\ x(x - 1) &= 0 \end{align}

So they intersect at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\). For the region, \(x\) goes from 0 to 1.

When rotating around the y-axis, the outer radius is the function farther from the y-axis, which is \(r_{outer} = x\), and the inner radius is \(r_{inner} = \sqrt{y}\) (solving for \(x\) in \(y = x^2\)).

We need to integrate with respect to \(y\), so our bounds are from \(y = 0\) to \(y = 1\).

\begin{align} V &= \pi \int_{0}^{1} [(y) - (\sqrt{y})^2] \, dy\\ &= \pi \int_{0}^{1} [y - y] \, dy\\ &= \pi \int_{0}^{1} 0 \, dy\\ &= 0 \end{align}

Wait, that can't be right. Let me reconsider the problem...

The outer function is \(x = y\) (solving for \(x\) in \(y = x\)), and the inner function is \(x = \sqrt{y}\).

\begin{align} V &= \pi \int_{0}^{1} [y^2 - (\sqrt{y})^2] \, dy\\ &= \pi \int_{0}^{1} [y^2 - y] \, dy\\ &= \pi \left[ \frac{y^3}{3} - \frac{y^2}{2} \right]_{0}^{1}\\ &= \pi \left( \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{2} \right)\\ &= \pi \left( \frac{2-3}{6} \right)\\ &= -\frac{\pi}{6} \approx -0.524 \end{align}

This negative result indicates I've made an error in setting up the problem. Let me redo this using parametric integration.

For rotation around the y-axis, we use:

\[ V = 2\pi \int_{a}^{b} x \cdot f(x) \, dx \]

where \(f(x)\) is the height of the region at point \(x\).

\begin{align} V &= 2\pi \int_{0}^{1} x \cdot (x - x^2) \, dx\\ &= 2\pi \int_{0}^{1} (x^2 - x^3) \, dx\\ &= 2\pi \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{4} \right]_{0}^{1}\\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} \right)\\ &= 2\pi \cdot \frac{4-3}{12}\\ &= 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{12}\\ &= \frac{\pi}{6} \approx 0.524 \end{align}
